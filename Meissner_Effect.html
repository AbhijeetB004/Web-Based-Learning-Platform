Meissner Effect

Meissner effect It was observed by Meissner and Ochsenfeld that, when a superconductor is cooled below its critical temperature (Tc), it undergoes a phase transition and enters a superconducting state. In this state, the superconductor exhibits perfect diamagnetism, meaning it repels any external magnetic field from its interior. This expulsion of magnetic flux is a characteristic feature of the Meissner effect. In a perfect diamagnetic material, we know that: B = μ₀(H + M) where, B represents the magnetic induction, H the magnetic field intensity, M the magnetization vector and μ₀ the permittivity of free space. We can express magnetization vector M (M = magnetic dipole moment per unit volume) in the following form, for linear medium: M = χH χ is +ve for paramagnetic like Aluminum, Platinum and -ve for diamagnetic like gold, silver, copper. Where, χ is the magnetic susceptibility. Substituting the value of magnetization vector M in the equation, one gets the following expression: B = μ₀(H + M) = μ₀(H + χH) = μ₀(1 + χ)H As we have observed for a superconducting sample, the magnetic field is zero inside the specimen, that is, 0 = μ₀(1 + χ)H

When an external magnetic field is applied to a superconductor, the magnetic flux tends to penetrate the material. However, the Cooper pairs actively respond to the magnetic field by forming currents that generate an opposing magnetic field. These induced currents create a magnetic field that perfectly cancels out the external magnetic field within the bulk of the superconductor. As a result, the magnetic field is expelled from the interior of the superconductor, confining it to the exterior regions as shown in figure 2. It's important to note that the Meissner effect is only observed in type I superconductors, which have a single critical temperature and exhibit a complete expulsion of the magnetic field. Type II superconductors, on the other hand, exhibit a mixed state where magnetic flux penetrates the material in the form of quantized vortices. In type II superconductors, the Meissner effect is limited to the region between these vortices
